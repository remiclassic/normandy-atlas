## Leinster exiles and the crossing

The twelfth-century invasion is a **political chain**, not a solitary battle: Diarmait Mac Murchada’s exile and recall, **Hiberno-Norse** ports as prizes, bilingual military households in his pay, Henry II’s **papal letter and Irish landing** (1171), and the slow consolidation of an **English lordship** that would outlast any one adventurer. Historians usually say **Anglo-Norman** — ethnic labels in the sources mix Irish, English, Welsh, and French affiliations.

## Ports and castles

**Wexford** marks an early landing and bridgehead. **Waterford** and **Dublin** were major **Hiberno-Norse** towns whose capture redrew trade and tribute. **Trim** embodies huge **comital** ambition in Meath. **Carrickfergus** belongs to the **Ulster** expansion under John de Courcy. Read each pin beside the **Irish annals** and Anglo-Norman administrative records — they rarely tell the same story in the same words.

## Dublin as colonial capital

**Dublin Castle** (from 1204) symbolizes royal oversight of the colony; its absence on an eleventh-century map is as informative as its presence after 1200. Use the era strip to avoid reading twelfth-century Ireland entirely through thirteenth-century buildings.
