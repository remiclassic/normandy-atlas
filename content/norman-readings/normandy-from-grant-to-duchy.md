## From Viking grant to ducal state

Modern scholarship treats the “foundation” of Normandy as a long process: Scandinavian war-bands, Frankish political bargains, and ecclesiastical integration, not a single treaty line on a map. Rouen’s prominence, the archbishopric, and the ducal court’s fiscal reach made the lower Seine a coherent polity by the eleventh century—whatever one makes of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte in the sources.

## Ducal authority and the Church

Bishops and abbots were not ornaments: they were nodes of literacy, land, and legitimacy. The map’s Norman pins on Mont-Saint-Michel, Bayeux, and Avranches are reminders that “Norman” power was partly exercised through sees, reform, and patronage—always negotiated with neighbors (Bretons, Angevins, French kings).
