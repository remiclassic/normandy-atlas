## Plural rule under one crown

Roger II’s kingdom bundled Latin, Greek, and Arabic administrative habits; churches and palaces in Palermo, Cefalù, and Monreale display that synthesis in stone and mosaic. The Palazzo dei Normanni is a palimpsest: Islamic-era fabric adapted for a Latin court.

## Church and monarchy

Episcopal cities (Catania, Messina) and royal foundations (Monreale) show how ecclesiastical politics and royal prestige intertwined. The map gives coordinates; the chronicles and charters explain who paid, who consecrated, and who resisted.

## Ifriqiya and the wider sea

Norman naval power and Sicilian interests sometimes reached African shores—treat those episodes as connected but distinct from insular administration. See caveat-led place readings for Mahdia and Tunis.
